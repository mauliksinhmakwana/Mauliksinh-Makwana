Reclaiming Your Focus: How to 'Touch the Sky' in a Distracted World

By MAKWANA MAULIK • November 13, 2025 • 6 min read

Look around. At the cafe, in the office, even at the dinner table. Everyone is distracted. Our world, from the apps on our phones ("The Social Dilemma") to the very "bugs" in our brains ("Cognitive Biases"), is a giant machine designed to steal our most valuable resource: our attention.

In this new world, being "busy"—checking emails, scrolling feeds, and jumping between tasks—is the default. It's easy. And it gets you an average life. But what about "touching the sky"? What about building something great, mastering a difficult skill, or creating real value? That's impossible if your focus is broken into a thousand tiny pieces.

The truth is, in a world where everyone is distracted, the person who can focus has a superpower.

The Lie: "Shallow Work" is Productive

We've been taught to mistake "busyness" for "productivity." We answer an email in 30 seconds. We respond to a Slack message. We check a notification. This is Shallow Work. It's easy, it's non-stop, and it *feels* like we're doing a lot. But at the end of the day, have we created anything of real value?

The antidote is Deep Work. This is the skill of focusing without distraction on a cognitively demanding task. It’s when you’re "in the zone," and the world melts away. It's the state where you solve hard problems, write great code, or create real art. Shallow work keeps you afloat. Deep work is how you "touch the sky."

Your Focus Isn't a Gift, It's a Muscle

You aren't "born" a focused person. Focus is a skill you train, just like any other muscle. Here's how to start your training:

  • Schedule Your Focus: You schedule meetings, so why not schedule "deep work"? Block 90 minutes on your calendar. Title it "FOCUS: Do Not Disturb." This is a meeting with your own goals.
  • Kill Your Notifications: This is the single most important step. Turn off *all* non-human notifications on your phone and computer. No pings, no banners, no red badges. You check your apps when *you* decide, not when *they* decide.
  • Embrace Boredom: The next time you're in line or waiting for a friend, don't pull out your phone. Just be bored. Let your mind wander. Boredom is the "soil" where your brain's best ideas begin to grow.

Conclusion: The Distracted vs. The Focused

The new divide in the world isn't between the rich and the poor; it's between the Distracted and the Focused.

"In a world of constant distraction, the ability to do deep work is like a superpower. It's the key to unlocking your full potential."

If you want to live a life of reaction, of "busyness," of being a slave to the next ping, the path is easy. Everyone is on it. But if you want to build, create, and "touch the sky"—you have to choose a different path. You have to reclaim your focus. It's the hardest work there is, and it's the only work that matters.
